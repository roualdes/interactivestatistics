Introduction

The Bernoulli distribution is a simple and flexible statistical model capturing any event that has only two outcomes. In fact, the Bernoulli distribution is the basis of some very sophisticaed binary classifiers in machine learning.

There are two key features of the Bernoulli distribution. This distribution can only take on two values, commonly referred to as failure and success. Failure gets recorded as \(0\) and success as \(1\). The probability of a success has a constant probability \(p\). Because there are only two outcomes under this distribution, the probability of a failure is \(1 - p\).

To model a real world process with the Bernoulli distribution, the researcher is to identify a process that has only two outcomes. Label the outcome of interest, success, and the other outcome as a failure. If it's reasonable to believe that the probability of success is constant in time and/or space, then there's a reasonable chance the Bernoulli distribution will be an appropriate model.

Density Function

The Bernoulli distribution is one of the simpler distribution functions because the variable \(x\) can only take on two possible values \(0\) or \(1\). We write this mathematically as \(x \in \{0, 1\}\) and read this as \(x\) is in the set that consists of \(0\) and \(1\). The Bernoulli distribution function is \[ f(x) = p^x (1 - p)^{(1 - x)}. \]

Try on your own to evaluate the density function across both values that \(x\) can take on. Do you understand now why the value \(1\) is associated with the probability \(p\)?

Estimation

Estimating the population parameter \(p\) is as simple as adding up all the Bernoulli observations and dividing by the sample size. That is, \( \hat{p} = \sum_{n = 1}^N x_n \). This is exactly what the pink triangle in the interactive display above is representing.
