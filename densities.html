Introduction

Density functions play a prominent role in applied statistics, despite only existing in certain scenarios. For now, we'll ignore these special cases and focus on gaining an understanding of density functions themselves, instead of the greater context surrounding density functions.

The easiest way to think about density functions is as functions with a small set of properties. The properties that make an ordinary function \( f \) defined on a space \( S \) a density function are

  1. \( f : S \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \), that is \( f \) is real-valued,
  2. for any \( x \in S \), \( f(x) \geq 0 \), and
  3. \( \int_{S} f = 1 \).

I intentionally leave out the \( dx \) for a number of reasons. First, the meaning of \( dx \) is not clear, see the Section "Meaning of the symbol \( dx \)" on Wikipedia's page on the Integral or Munkres' "Analysis on Manifolds." Second, and more practically important for us, that integral absuses some more advanced notation Check out the Section General Distribution Functions in Kyle Siegrist's OER book Probability, Mathematical Statistics, and Stochastic Processes as hosted on LibreTexts.org.that says, when the space \( S \) is countable, the integral becomes a sum, and when that space \( S \) is uncountable the integral is really an integral.
