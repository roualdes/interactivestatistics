Introduction

Density functions play a prominent role in applied statistics, despite only existing in certain scenarios. For now, we'll ignore these special cases and focus on gaining an understanding of density functions themselves, instead of the greater context surrounding density functions.

The easiest way to think about density functions is as functions with a small set of properties. The properties that make an ordinary function \( f \) defined on a space \( S \) a density function are I use the phrase density function to refer to both probability mass functions and probability density functions.

  1. \( f \) is real-valued, \( f : S \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \),
  2. \( f \) is non-negative, for any \( x \in S \), \( f(x) \geq 0 \), and
  3. the area under the function is equal to one, \( \int_{S} f = 1 \).

I intentionally leave out the \( dx \) for a number of reasons. First, the meaning of \( dx \) is not obvious, see the Section "Meaning of the symbol \( dx \)" on Wikipedia's page on the Integral or Munkres' "Analysis on Manifolds." Second, and more practically important for us, that integral absuses some more advanced notation Check out the Section General Distribution Functions in Kyle Siegrist's OER book Probability, Mathematical Statistics, and Stochastic Processes as hosted on LibreTexts.org.that says, when the space \( S \) is countable, the integral becomes a sum, and when that space \( S \) is uncountable the integral is really an integral.

These lecture notes first introduce the two most common structures of sample spaces for density functions, discrete and continous. Some examples follow, where we describe a handful of scenarios where specific density functions are employed. The density functions highlighted all come from well known and named distributions. Last, we explore some properties of density functions.

Discrete Sample Spaces

The third property above depends on the structure of the space \( S \). When \( S \) is a countable set, say \( \mathbb{N} \), we have \[ \sum_{x \in S} f(x) = 1. \]

Let's try a simple example. Consider a possibly unfair coin, where we denote heads as one and tails as zero, so that \( S = \{0, 1\} \). In this case, we'd call \( f \) the density function of a Bernoulli distribution Check out my lecture notes on the family of Bernoulli distributions. with parameter \( p \) and has the following form \[ f(x | p) = p^x (1 - p) ^{1 - x}. \]

You should verify that all three properties above are met with the density function \( f(x | p) \) defined for arbitrary \( p \in (0, 1) \).

A unique feature of density functions on countable spaces is that probabilities of points are equal to the density function evaluated at that point, \( \mathbb{P}[\{x\}] = f(x) \). This property is not true for density functions defined on uncountable spaces.

Continuous Sample Spaces

When \( S \) is an uncountable space, say \( \mathbb{R} \), we have \[ \int_S f(x) dx. \]

Let's try an example. Consider the family of Exponential Distributions, defined on \( S = \mathbb{R}^+ \), where the density function depends on the population parameter \( \lambda \in \mathbb{R}^+ \) and has the form \[ f(x | \lambda) = \lambda \exp{(-\lambda x)}. \]

You should verify that all three properties above are met with the density function \( f(x | \lambda) \) defined for arbitrary \( \lambda \in \mathbb{R}^+ \).

Examples

Density functions are the basic building blocks of applied statisticians.Density functions are closely associated with named distribution functions. All the examples below will highlight density function which come from a named distribution. Their basic structure helps statisticians model the stochastic world with some relatively simple tricks.

Bernoulli Distribution. The family of Bernoulli distributions have a key characteristic that makes identifying when to use the density function of a Bernoulli distribution more obvious than most other distributions. The sample space \( S \) for a Bernoulli distribution has exactly two elements, which are often taken to be \( \{0, 1\} \) no matter what values the real world process produces. So long as there's only two outcomes, label the one that is more interesting one and the other zero.

The classic example for a Bernoulli distribution is a coin. The Bernoullid distribution has a single parameter \( p \), which statisticians to model the world by estimating \( p \) from data. Because the parameter \( p \) can be any value in \( (0, 1) \), the density function of a Bernoulli distribution is often used in more realistic scenarios. The US presidential elections are often modeled with a Bernoulli density function.

For a third example, imagine that you are interested in testing a specific version of your web-browser. You randomly sample \( 1000 \) web-pages and load them on your browser. If a particular page loads correctly, label it one. If a particular page does not load correctly, label it zero. These \( 1000 \) data points consisting of ones and zeros are called observations. The data theoretically came from a Bernoulli distribuiton with a value of \( p \) that is unknown.
